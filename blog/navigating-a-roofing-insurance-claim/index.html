How to Navigate a Roofing Insurance Claim (Without the Headache)

April 10, 2026  |  Redefine Roofing Team

A roofing insurance claim is a process with rules, deadlines, and paperwork — and in Florida, several of those rules changed in recent years. Handled correctly, it's very manageable. Handled late or loosely, it can cost you real money. Here's the step-by-step path we walk homeowners through every storm season.

Step 1: Document First, Touch Nothing

As soon as it's safe after a storm, photograph everything: visible roof damage from the ground, fallen debris, interior stains or drips, and damaged belongings. Time-stamped phone photos are fine. Don't throw away damaged materials yet, and don't make permanent repairs before the damage is documented — temporary protection is expected and encouraged, but permanent repairs before documentation can complicate the claim.

Step 2: Prevent Further Damage (Your Policy Requires It)

Nearly every policy obligates you to take reasonable steps to prevent additional damage after a loss. That's what emergency tarping is: fast, temporary protection that stops water intrusion and shows your carrier you acted responsibly. Keep every receipt — reasonable mitigation costs are typically reimbursable under the claim. We offer same-day emergency tarping across all six counties we serve, 24/7.

Step 3: Get an Independent Professional Inspection

Before or immediately after reporting the claim, have a licensed local roofer inspect and document the roof. This matters for two reasons. First, storm damage — wind-broken sealant bonds, hail bruising, lifted flashing — is often invisible from the ground; you need to know the real scope before anyone assigns a number to it. Second, a detailed photo record from a professional gives your claim a factual foundation and gives you a second data point beside the adjuster's assessment.

Step 4: Report the Claim — Promptly

Florida law currently gives homeowners one year from the date of loss to report a new claim (and 18 months for supplemental claims). That's the legal maximum, not a suggestion — in practice, fresher claims are easier to attribute and settle. Report through your carrier's claims line or app, provide your documentation, and note your claim number.

Step 5: Meet the Adjuster — With Your Roofer Present

Your insurer will send an adjuster to assess the damage. This is the single most important appointment in the process, and you shouldn't do it alone: have your roofing contractor on the roof with the adjuster. A good contractor points out every legitimate item of storm damage so nothing is missed, answers technical questions about repair scope and code requirements, and keeps the conversation factual. We do this for our customers as standard practice.

Step 6: Review the Estimate and Understand Your Payout

The carrier's estimate will reflect either replacement cost value (RCV) or actual cash value (ACV — replacement cost minus depreciation), depending on your policy and your roof's age. You'll pay your deductible; on RCV policies, recoverable depreciation is typically released after the work is completed. If the carrier's scope misses documented damage, a supplemental claim with supporting evidence is the normal, legitimate path to correcting it.

Know the Florida-Specific Rules

  • Your deductible is yours by law. A contractor offering to waive, absorb, or "creatively finance" your hurricane deductible is proposing insurance fraud — and homeowners share the legal exposure. Walk away.
  • Assignment of Benefits (AOB) is gone. For current policies, Florida law no longer allows contractors to take assignment of your claim benefits. You stay in control of your claim — treat anyone asking you to sign over benefits as a red flag.
  • Only licensed professionals can negotiate your claim. Your contractor documents damage and speaks to technical scope; negotiating the claim itself is limited by law to you, your attorney, or a licensed public adjuster.
  • Verify every contractor's license at myfloridalicense.com before signing anything. Ours is CCC 1331457.

Step 7: Choose Your Contractor Like You'll Need Them in Five Years

Because you might. Warranty service only exists if the company still exists — and still answers the phone in your area code. Choose local, licensed, and established: verify the license, check reviews and BBB standing, confirm a physical local address, and get a detailed written scope. Redefine Roofing has been restoring Tampa Bay roofs since 2010 from our Largo headquarters, with a lifetime workmanship warranty behind every replacement and repair — in Pinellas, Hillsborough, and all six counties we call home.

What If the Claim Is Denied?

A denial isn't necessarily the end. Common reasons include damage attributed to wear rather than the storm, late reporting, or an adjuster who simply missed items. Your options: request a re-inspection with your contractor's documentation in hand, file a supplemental claim with additional evidence, invoke your policy's appraisal process if it has one, or consult a licensed public adjuster or attorney for a disputed claim. Good documentation from day one is what keeps these options open — another reason the professional inspection in Step 3 isn't optional.

The Short Version

Photograph everything, tarp fast and keep receipts, get a professional inspection, report within the deadline, put your roofer on the roof with the adjuster, and never sign with anyone who plays games with deductibles.
